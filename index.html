About Me

I am a data scientist and interdisciplinary quantitative researcher, currently studying in the Statistics MA program at Columbia University. My interests are in causal inference, experimentation, and machine learning. I also work in quantitative research at Eurasia Group, a political risk consulting firm, where I contribute methodological expertise and support data science efforts.

Prior to graduate school, I was a Fulbright-Nehru Research Fellow in India, where I studied state-level elections and voter behavior. I was affiliated with the Trivedi Centre for Political Data at Ashoka University, and spent time at the Centre for Policy Research. I performed quantitative analysis, built a dataset from archival work, and conducted interviews of Members of Parliament and Legislative Assemblies in Gujarat.

I have also worked as a data scientist on a research and development team within the Department of Revenue at the Commonwealth of Massachusetts, a software developer at MIT, and as a technical intern at Evidence for Policy Design, housed within the Harvard Center for International Development.

Projects

NYC Taxi Random Forest Classifier

Using the NYC public taxi data, the project analyzes data from the three different types of rides (yellow taxis, green taxis, and for-hire rides) and uses a randome forest classifiers to predict which borough a ride will end in. I used data from January 2018 for each ride type. The yellow taxi model performs at about 85% accuracy, the green taxi model performs at about 80% accuracy, and the for-hire rides perform at about 65-70% accuracy.

Transportability and External Validity in Evaluating Women’s Political Reservation Effects in India

Ongoing: This project aims to examine the generalizability of causal effects obtained from a randomized controlled trial (RCT), specifically, the “Women as Policy Makers” (Duflo et. al.) study in India, to a new target population. Using the structural causal model (SCM) framework, the project will build a formal SCM for the RCT data. Next, methods from transportability and external validity, for example selection diagrams, will determine whether the estimated effect (e.g., the impact of reserved political positions on local public good investments) can be identified in a new population that differs in key covariates (such as socio-economic characteristics or cultural factors). The project focuses on generalizability, robustness, and adaptation. Using data from the RCT as well as additional relevant data from external sources, the project aims to bridge the gap between rigorous experimental findings and policy decisions in varied real-world environments.

Inferential Analysis of Citi Bike Ride Patterns

The primary goal of the project is to explore relationships between building energy efficiency and sustainable transportation. At a high level I was interested in whether certain types of people take or have the option to choose sustainable transportation, and generally in what contexts they do so. The case study is New York City, and I utilized inferential procedures, relying on linear regression models, to study more specifically relationships between building energy usage, Citi Bike ride patterns, and neighborhood socioeconomic and housing data. These data not only provide fertile ground for applying statistical techniques from the course, but also lay the groundwork for further research with important policy implications. Columbia Data Science Institute Scholar

This project, Mapping Afro-Brazilian Religious Histories: An AI-Assisted Tutorial, examines the historical and spatial dimensions of witchcraft accusations in Rio de Janeiro during the late 19th and early 20th centuries. By combining archival research with data science techniques, such as web scraping, natural language processing (NLP), and geospatial mapping, the project helps to explore how accusations of witchcraft were used to reinforce racial and class divides. Using the addresses of accused witches, the project aims to visualize how these trials mapped onto the city, reinforcing social and spatial segregation. We started the process with a glossary of about 60 relevant terms and performed searches across newspapers including Jornal do Brasil, Gazeta de Notícias, Diário de Pernambuco, and Diário da Noite.

Investigating Voter Turnout in Indian Elections

The purpose of this analysis is to use Bayesian multilevel models to explore potential factors of why voter turnout tends to be higher in local and state-level elections vs. national elections. India generally has a comparatively higher turnout rate, and is unique in that voter turnout increases as each level of election decreases. The United States and Europe, where most of the turnout research has been conducted, face the opposite effects. I used election data from 1967-2021, and explored some potential explanations like which type of parties win at what rates of turnout, if there is an effect in the timing of state and national elections, and what regional differences exist.
